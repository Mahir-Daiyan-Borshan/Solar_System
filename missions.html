Sputnik 1

October 4, 1957

On October 4, 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, the first artificial satellite to orbit Earth, and in doing so kicked off the Space Age. The small, polished metal sphere (weighing about 83.6 kg) with four antennae transmitted a simple radio "beep-beep" that was heard around the world, yet its impact was enormous.

First artificial Earth satellite

Marked the beginning of the Space Age

Transmitted radio signals for 21 days

It orbited every ~96 minutes and remained active for about 22 days until battery depletion, finally re-entering Earth's atmosphere on January 4, 1958.

Vostok 1

April 12, 1961

On April 12, 1961, the Soviet spacecraft Vostok 1 launched from the Baikonur Cosmodrome with cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin aboard, making him the first human to travel into space and orbit the Earth. The flight lasted about 108 minutes and completed one orbit around the planet.

Mercury-Redstone 3

On 5 May 1961, the United States launched Mercury-Redstone 3 (often called Freedom 7) with astronaut Alan Shepard Jr. aboard, marking the first American human spaceflight. Though it was a sub-orbital flight lasting about 15 minutes and reaching roughly 116 miles (187 km) in altitude, the mission achieved a fundamental milestone: it demonstrated that a human could endure the stresses of launch and re-entry and return safely. Shepard’s capsule, Freedom 7, was launched atop a modified Redstone rocket, and the flight illustrated the U.S. commitment in the early space race to close the gap with the Soviet Union. Next Spaceflight Though much shorter than the earlier Soviet orbital flight of Vostok 1, it spurred U.S. confidence and escalated human spaceflight efforts. The flight also allowed engineers and mission planners to test life-support, capsule control and human physiological responses in brief spaceflight. For your timeline site, Mercury-Redstone 3 represents the first American human spaceflight — a “first step” for NASA in the human space exploration era.

Vostok 6

On 16 June 1963 the Soviet Union launched Vostok 6, carrying cosmonaut Valentina Tereshkova, who became the first woman in space. The solo flight lasted nearly 3 days (2 days, 22 hours, 50 minutes) during which Tereshkova completed 48 orbits of Earth. Her mission was both a technical achievement and a symbolic one: it gathered valuable biomedical data on how a female body handled weightlessness, rotation and re-entry, and it reinforced Soviet prestige in the space race. The Vostok 6 spacecraft also flew at a steep inclination of ~65°, a record for human spaceflight at the time. spacefacts.de While the spacecraft was mechanically similar to previous Vostoks, this mission emphasized the expansion of human diversity in space. For your timeline, Vostok 6 highlights the broadening of human spaceflight beyond just “first human” to “first woman” — a critical milestone.

Apollo 8

Launched on 21 December 1968, Apollo 8 was a pivotal U.S. spaceflight mission: the first crewed spacecraft to leave low Earth orbit, travel to the Moon, orbit it ten times, and safely return to Earth. The crew — Frank Borman, Jim Lovell and William Anders — witnessed the far side of the Moon firsthand and captured the now-iconic “Earthrise” image, showing Earth as a small blue marble rising above the lunar horizon. The mission’s success validated the performance of the Saturn V launch vehicle and the Apollo command/service module in translunar flight. Politically and scientifically, Apollo 8 shifted human spaceflight from Earth orbit to deep space — from orbiting our planet to orbiting another celestial body. For your timeline site this mission represents the threshold of human interplanetary travel: no landing, but a full lunar orbital mission.

Apollo 11

On July 20, 1969 the Apollo 11 mission achieved what had been envisioned since the dawn of the space age: humans walking on another celestial body. Launched on July 16 aboard a Saturn V rocket, the crew of Commander Neil Armstrong, Lunar Module Pilot Buzz Aldrin and Command Module Pilot Michael Collins entered lunar orbit, then Armstrong and Aldrin descended in the Lunar Module “Eagle” and landed in the Sea of Tranquility. Two hours and about 15 minutes later Armstrong stepped onto the lunar surface, declaring “that’s one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind.” They collected lunar samples (~21 kg), deployed experiments, and then lifted off to rendezvous with Collins for the return to Earth, splashing down on July 24. For your timeline, Apollo 11 marks the first time humans walked on the Moon — a watershed moment in human space exploration, symbolizing the culmination of the race to the Moon and opening the way for broader deep-space ambitions.

Salyut 1

Launched on April 19 1971, Salyut 1 was the world’s first space station, operated by the Soviet Union. The station weighed about 20 tons and provided a new paradigm: instead of brief flights in orbit, humans would now live and work in space for extended periods. Though the first crew to dock (Soyuz 10) was unable to board, the experience of Salyut laid the groundwork for long-duration habitation in space. The concept of a modular, orbiting laboratory where crews could live and experiment changed the trajectory of human spaceflight. In your timeline, Salyut 1 stands for the shift from simple orbital flights to sustained human presence in space.

Apollo-Soyuz Test Project

In July 1975 the Apollo–Soyuz Test Project achieved the first international human spaceflight docking mission: a U.S. Apollo spacecraft docked with a Soviet Soyuz in orbit. The joint mission was a major symbolic and technical milestone: it validated a universal docking mechanism, fostered cooperation during the Cold War era, and paved the way for future international partnerships in space exploration. For your timeline, this mission represents the turning point from rivalry to collaboration in human spaceflight.

Space Shuttle Columbia

Launched on April 12 1981, STS-1 was the inaugural flight of NASA’s Space Shuttle program, with the orbiter Columbia carrying astronauts John Young and Robert Crippen. Its key mission: to demonstrate that a reusable spacecraft could safely launch, orbit, return and land like an airplane. It completed 37 orbits in about 2 days and highlighted the shift toward routine access to space and reusability. On your timeline, STS-1 signifies the start of a new era of spaceflight: the “spaceplane” era.

Mir Space Station

Launched as its first module in 1986, Mir became the first modular, continuously-inhabited space station, operating under Soviet then Russian control. Over its operational life, Mir hosted decades of international crews, research in microgravity, and set records for long-duration human spaceflight. For your timeline, Mir embodies the maturation of human space habitation and the bridge between early space stations and today’s orbiting laboratory.

Hubble Space Telescope

Launched on April 24 1990 aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery, the Hubble Space Telescope transformed our view of the Universe. By operating above Earth’s atmosphere, Hubble captured unprecedented images of galaxies, nebulae and cosmic phenomena, contributing massively to astrophysics and our understanding of the cosmos. While not a human‐flight mission per se, it changed human space exploration by the science accessible from orbit and the human capacity to observe the Universe. On your timeline, Hubble marks the scientific leap from exploration to understanding.

International Space Station

Launched initially on November 20, 1998, the ISS has been continuously inhabited by human crews since November 2000. It is a multinational orbital laboratory, shared by NASA, Roscosmos, ESA, JAXA and others, and serves as the platform for long‐duration human spaceflight, research in microgravity, and preparation for future deep space missions. In your timeline, the ISS stands for the matured cooperation and sustained presence of humans in space.

Shenzhou 5

LOn October 15 2003 China launched Shenzhou 5, carrying taikonaut Yang Liwei and becoming the third nation (after USSR/Russia and USA) to send a human into space. The flight lasted about 21 hours and completed 14 orbits, achieving China’s entry into crewed spaceflight and marking a significant milestone in global space exploration. In your timeline, Shenzhou 5 highlights the expanding human presence in space beyond the initial superpowers.

SpaceX Falcon 9 + Dragon

On May 30 2020 the Crew Dragon Demo-2 mission launched NASA astronauts Doug Hurley and Bob Behnken aboard a SpaceX Crew Dragon spacecraft from U.S. soil, the first American crewed orbital launch since 2011, and the first operational commercial human spaceflight. It docked to the ISS and demonstrated that commercial partners could reliably transport humans to orbit, opening a new chapter in human spaceflight. In your timeline, this mission represents the commercial era of human space exploration.

Artemis I

Launched on November 16 2022, Artemis I was an uncrewed test flight of NASA’s Orion spacecraft and the Space Launch System rocket, sending Orion into a lunar flyby trajectory and back to Earth after about 25 days. Though no crew was on board, the mission validated critical systems for future human lunar missions and represents the next frontier: returning humans to the Moon and beyond. On your timeline, Artemis I signifies the transition to deep-space human missions and the return to lunar exploration.
